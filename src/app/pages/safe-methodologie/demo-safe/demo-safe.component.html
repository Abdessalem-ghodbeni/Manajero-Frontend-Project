Presentation of the SAFe Agile Methodology

SAFe -Methodology

What Is SAFe

The Scaled Agile Framework (SAFe) is a methodology that helps large organizations adopt agile principles at scale.
SAFe Scrum is an Agile method used by teams within an Agile Release Train (ART) to deliver customer value in a short period of time. SAFe Scrum teams use iterations, Kanban systems, and Scrum events to plan, execute, demonstrate, and reflect on their work. Many teams use SAFe Scrum as their primary Agile team process, but they are not limited to Scrum and may also use SAFe Team Kanban. However, all SAFe teams apply integrated quality techniques and other practices and activities not defined in Scrum. SAFe Scrum teams collaborate with other Agile teams and ART stakeholders, creating and deploying solutions that benefit their customers. This article describes how Agile teams apply SAFe Scrum.

More...

Agile teams applying SAFe Scrum follow a regular cadence of events to achieve a common goal, delivering value to the business and its customers. These teams have the authority and autonomy needed to plan, execute, and manage their work, make decisions within their scope, and adapt to changing conditions in the best way they see fit. Advantages

The SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework) methodology offers several notable advantages for organizations seeking to adopt agility at scale:

  • Facilitates collaboration among large teams
  • Enhances transparency and visibility of projects
  • Enables better management of dependencies
  • Promotes continuous improvement

Disadvantages

Despite its many advantages, SAFe also has some drawbacks that need to be considered:

  • Can be complex to implement initially
  • Requires significant commitment from management
  • Can lead to rigidity if poorly applied
  • Can be costly in terms of training and resources

SAFe Lifecycle

The following sections provide an overview of this cycle.

Team Backlog
The Team Backlog contains all upcoming work necessary to advance the solution. Most of the work is captured by user stories, but other tasks and activities (e.g., training, support) can also be included. The team backlog is partially filled during PI Planning, and teams establish their PI objectives. Teams continuously refine the backlog to ensure it always contains stories ready to be implemented without significant risk or surprises. During backlog refinement, teams review upcoming user stories and features (if any) to define, discuss, estimate, and establish acceptance criteria for the next backlog items. Backlog refinement is an ongoing process throughout the iteration.
the Sprints
Sprints
are the heartbeat of Scrum and create a work rhythm within the larger PI timeframe. All the work necessary to complete the iteration, including planning, team synchronization, iteration review, and retrospective, is carried out within the iterations. Each iteration is a standard, fixed-length period, usually lasting two weeks. During the iteration, the team delivers high-quality value increments in the form of functional and tested software, solutions, or other valuable artifacts. Iterations are continuous and sequential, with a new iteration beginning immediately after the previous one.
Sprint Planning
What is Sprint Planning?

Sprint Planning is the first event of the iteration, typically facilitated by the Scrum Master or Team Coach. All team members collaborate to determine which parts of the team backlog can be completed during the upcoming iteration. The resulting plan is recorded in the iteration backlog.

Participants and Preparation
  • Product Owner: Ensures that participants are ready to discuss the most critical backlog items and their alignment with sprint goals and PI objectives.
  • Scrum Team: May invite others for additional guidance.
Sprint Planning Objectives
  • Sprint Value: Why is this sprint valuable?
  • Sprint Content: What can be accomplished in this sprint?
  • Work Planning: How will the work be carried out and completed?
Process
  • Acceptance Criteria: The team develops acceptance criteria for each User Story.
  • Effort Estimation: Estimates the effort needed to complete each story.
  • Story Selection: Selects candidate stories based on the available capacity for the sprint.
  • Work Planning: Plans the necessary work to create a value increment that meets the Definition of Done (DoD).
  • Decomposition of Items: Breaks down backlog items into user stories or tasks that can be completed in a day or two.
  • Team Commitment: The team commits to the work and records the sprint backlog in a visible location (storyboard, Kanban board, Agile project management tool).
Time Limit

The planning is limited to a maximum of approximately two hours for a two-week sprint.

Summary
In summary, sprint planning in SAFe is a collaborative and structured process aimed at defining and organizing the team's work for the upcoming sprint, while ensuring alignment with sprint goals and PI objectives.

Creating Objectives

After planning the iteration and establishing the corresponding backlog, the Agile team synthesizes the iteration objectives. These objectives are derived from the backlog and aligned with the broader goals of the Program Increment (PI).

Sprint Backlog

During sprint planning, teams select tasks to be completed from the sprint backlog by extracting items from the team backlog. This sprint backlog is regularly adjusted, as PI planning is overarching and requires adaptations for each sprint. Teams receive feedback from their past sprints, as well as demonstrations from the system and other collaborating teams. Local concerns such as defects, technical debt, and maintenance, as well as delays at the ART level and committed PI objectives, also influence the content of the sprint backlog.

Delivery

In SAFe, Scrum teams adhere to the rhythms and synchronizations of the Agile Release Train (ART), which facilitates alignment, dependency management, and rapid learning cycles for the entire solution. During a sprint, each team collaborates to define, build, and test the planned stories. They monitor the sprint's progress, improve value flow using Kanban boards, and participate in team synchronization events. Teams deliver stories throughout the sprint and avoid delays. They apply quality practices to build the system correctly. Completed stories are showcased during regular iteration demonstrations and reviews.

Team Synchronization

Team Synchronization is a short daily meeting (typically 15 minutes or less) to assess progress towards iteration goals, communicate, and adjust upcoming work.
The team may use various structures or techniques during this synchronization to establish an action plan for coordinating the day's work. However, this meeting is not the only time adjustments can be made: the team collaborates and discusses necessary adaptations or replanning at any time. High-performing teams use this synchronization to identify opportunities for mutual support in successfully achieving their iteration goals. The Scrum Master or Team Coach takes note of topics requiring further discussion on a dedicated "parking lot" board. Only relevant parties stay behind to discuss these topics in more detail. Ineffective team synchronizations can reveal deeper issues requiring a systemic approach to resolution, often under the responsibility of the Scrum Master or Team Coach.

The illustration below shows how new features of the solution evolve with each sprint. Each increment builds upon those that came before it and is verified to ensure they function well together.

Each increment is a functional, tested, and usable solution that meets the quality criteria defined in the Definition of Done (DoD). The team may deliver multiple increments during a single sprint, and the entirety is inspected during the sprint review for validation.

Agile Sprint Review

For Scrum teams, the sprint review is a crucial event where each team inspects the outcomes of the sprint. They present their work to key stakeholders and assess progress towards sprint goals and PI objectives.

The team also determines future adaptations to the product or process based on the review outcomes. Stakeholders and the team collaborate on the way forward, potentially adjusting the Team Backlog to address new opportunities. The iteration review is time-boxed to a maximum of 90 minutes for a typical two-week iteration.

Sprint Retrospective

The Sprint Retrospective is the final event of the sprint. Its main objective is to reflect on the sprint and derive new ideas to improve its process and the solution. The retrospective contributes to instilling the concept of continuous improvement, a core value in SAFe.

During the retrospective, the team discusses what went well, issues encountered, and how these issues were (or were not) resolved. They identify the most useful changes to improve, address promptly, or record in the backlog for the next sprint. The retrospective is limited to a maximum of approximately 60 minutes for a typical two-week sprint.

Participating in the System Demo

The system demo provides a comprehensive view of the new features delivered in the last sprint by all teams on the Agile Release Train (ART). Each demo allows all ART members to objectively assess the progress made during the Program Increment (PI).

All ART members participate or attend the system demonstration, ensuring a collective vision and sharing of the advancements achieved.

Rôles SAFe Scrum
  • La taille de l'équipe Scrum (généralement dix personnes ou moins) optimise la communication, l'interaction et la capacité à apporter de la valeur.
  • "L'équipe Scrum est suffisamment petite pour rester agile et suffisamment grande pour accomplir un travail important en un sprint."
  • Toutes les équipes SAFe Agile, y compris celles qui utilisent Scrum, ont deux rôles d'équipe spécialisés :
    • Le Scrum Master/Team Coach (parfois à temps partiel) coordonne le flux de travail, encourageant un environnement propice à la haute performance et à l'amélioration continue.
    • Le Product Owner (généralement à temps plein) comprend les besoins des clients, priorise le backlog de l'équipe et assure que le bon travail est réalisé de la bonne manière.
Les Équipes dans le Train Agile

Bien que les équipes soient interfonctionnelles, une seule équipe Agile peut ne pas être en mesure de fournir une valeur complète à l'utilisateur final pour de grands systèmes incluant différentes plates-formes technologiques et disciplines variées comme l'ingénierie matérielle, logicielle et système.

Les équipes doivent s’efforcer de fournir une valeur de bout en bout de manière la plus indépendante possible afin d’optimiser les flux.

Au sein de l'Agile Release Train (ART), toutes les équipes planifient, font des démonstrations et apprennent ensemble, facilitant l'alignement et la collaboration pour créer des solutions plus vastes.

L'ART permet de réduire ou éliminer les dépendances et d'améliorer le flux d'équipe et d'ART, favorisant l'exploration, l'intégration, le déploiement et la libération de valeur de manière plus indépendante.

Conclusion sur SAFe

SAFe offre une approche structurée pour l'adoption Agile à grande échelle, alignant stratégie et opérations à travers différents niveaux de l'organisation.

En intégrant des principes Lean-Agile, SAFe favorise la livraison continue de valeur client, améliorant la qualité et la prévisibilité des livraisons.

Les rôles définis et les événements structurés encouragent l'auto-organisation des équipes tout en facilitant la coordination et la gestion des dépendances.

SAFe promeut l'innovation continue par la réflexion régulière, l'amélioration des processus et l'adaptation rapide aux changements du marché.

Ce cadre aide les organisations à devenir plus flexibles, réactives et orientées vers la valeur client, renforçant leur compétitivité dans un environnement dynamique.
